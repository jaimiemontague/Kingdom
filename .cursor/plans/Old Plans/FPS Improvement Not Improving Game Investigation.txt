  Entities visually lurch every 3 frames then freeze for 2 frames — a clearly perceivable 20 Hz stutter even when the
  FPS counter shows 60. The lerp was designed to close the remaining gap across frames 2 and 3, but sync_key prevents it
   from ever running on those frames because pos_xyz hasn't changed.

  Animation frame changes occasionally break the sync_key match and let the lerp sneak in, but typical pixel art runs at
   6-12 FPS (frame change every 5-10 render frames), so most intermediate frames still get early-returned.

  Problem 2: The FPS counter measures the wrong thing

  render_coordinator.py:232 uses pygame.time.Clock.get_fps() — an internal EMA that smooths over ~1 second. The Ursina
  path uses prev * 0.92 + inst * 0.08 (line 1165 of ursina_app.py), which takes ~28 frames to represent half of any
  change.

  Both metrics show average frame rate, which hides the thing humans actually feel: frame time variance. A game at 55
  FPS with periodic 100ms hitches reads "55 FPS" on the counter but feels terrible. A perfectly steady 30 FPS reads "30
  FPS" but feels smooth. The FPS counter literally cannot distinguish these scenarios.

  Problem 3: The lerp formula itself creates non-uniform motion

  Even without the sync_key bug, alpha = 0.82 as a constant blend factor creates exponentially decaying movement per
  frame:
  - Frame 1 after tick: 82% of distance
  - Frame 2: 14.8% of distance
  - Frame 3: 2.7% of distance

  This is a "lurch then crawl" pattern. True interpolation should use sim_accumulator / FIXED_SIM_DT as a linear blend
  between previous and current sim positions, producing uniform motion between ticks.

  Problem 4: Performance metrics are from headless tests

  The reported improvements ("0.98ms avg tick, P95 2.13ms") come from headless stress tests that only measure sim cost.
  The actual bottleneck is render-side work (Panda3D traversal, Python sync loops) which the headless tests don't touch.
   The metrics look great on paper but describe a different workload than what the player experiences.

  ---
  Proposal: Smoothness Monitor

  I'd suggest replacing the current FPS overlay with a frame-time focused monitor that measures what humans actually
  perceive. Here's what I'd include:

  1. Frame Time Bar Graph (most valuable)

  A scrolling horizontal bar chart of the last 120 frames. Each bar = one frame's total time in ms, color-coded:
  - Green: <16.67ms (60+ FPS)
  - Yellow: 16.67–33ms (30–60 FPS)
  - Red: >33ms (below 30 FPS)

  This makes spikes, periodic patterns (like the 20 Hz sim tick), and GC pauses immediately visible as tall bars in the
  graph.

  2. Frame Time Percentiles

  Rolling 5-second window showing:
  - P50 (median): what most frames look like
  - P95 (1-in-20): noticeable hitches
  - P99 (1-in-100): worst-case freezes
  - 1% Low FPS: industry-standard "perceived smoothness" metric — average of the worst 1% of frames

  The gap between P50 and P95 directly quantifies "jitter."

  3. Sim/Render Split Indicator

  Show per-frame whether a sim tick ran, and the time breakdown:
  - SIM: 1.2ms | SYNC: 2.4ms | RENDER: 5.1ms | TOTAL: 8.7ms

  This reveals whether the sim/render decoupling is working and which stage is spiking.

  4. Frame Pacing Dot

  A small colored circle: green when the last frame was within ±25% of target frame time, red otherwise. Rapid
  flickering = inconsistent pacing = perceived stutter.

  ---
  Recommended Fix Priority

  Before building the monitor, the interpolation bug is worth fixing first since it's the most likely cause of the
  "nothing feels smoother" perception:

  1. Fix the sync_key vs lerp conflict — either exclude pos_xyz from the sync_key (so the lerp can continue closing the
  gap on frames without sim ticks), or move the lerp computation before the early-out
  2. Switch from exponential lerp to linear interpolation — use sim_accumulator / FIXED_SIM_DT to blend linearly between
   prev_sim_pos and curr_sim_pos
  3. Build the smoothness monitor — to actually verify that future fixes are improving perceived smoothness, not just
  average FPS


---
  What Was Fixed

  The twitching/jittering bug was caused by the interpolation window never converging. When _sync_unit_atlas_billboard
  received the same pos_xyz on consecutive frames (entity not moving), the prev/curr window stayed frozen — prev at the
  entity's old position, curr at its current position. As the blend fraction cycled (0.2 → 0.9 → 0.2 each sim tick
  period), the visual position oscillated between prev + 20% of gap and prev + 90% of gap.

  For heroes in buildings: the hero teleports from their walking position to the building center — prev stayed at the
  walking position forever, creating 100-200px of vertical jitter as blend cycled.

  For peasants/tax collector: entity separation pushes by small amounts each tick, then they stand still. prev stayed at
   the pre-push position, creating visible twitching.

  The fix adds sim_tick_id — a monotonic counter incremented per sim tick. The renderer now advances prev = curr, curr =
   new_pos on every tick boundary, regardless of whether position actually changed. When an entity is stationary, prev
  == curr, and interpolation produces a stable position no matter what blend is.

  Performance Analysis Ideas

  Based on the code and your 11-13 FPS 1% Low reading:

  1. The Panda3D scene graph traversal is still the dominant bottleneck. Round 3's frustum culling reduced active nodes
  from 16k-44k to 2k-4k, but even 2k-4k nodes costs 3-6ms of pure Panda3D traversal. Combined with Python sync loops
  (1-3ms), fog/tree updates (0-2ms), and sim ticks (0-2ms), you're at 6-13ms best case — which is 77-166 FPS
  theoretical. But the 1% Low at 11-13 FPS means something is spiking individual frames to 75-90ms.
  2. Likely spike sources: The smoothness monitor bar graph should reveal the pattern. Things to look for:
    - Periodic tall red bars every N frames → throttled systems running on their duty frame (tree sync every 4th,
  separation every 2nd) all aligning on the same frame
    - Random isolated spikes → Python GC pauses or Panda3D internal reallocation
    - Sustained blocks of yellow/red → camera panning into dense areas triggering mass entity enable/disable from
  frustum culling
  3. The Phase 3 terrain flattening from the Round 3 plan (merging static grass/path/rock entities into chunked meshes
  via flatten_strong()) was marked as conditional. If the bar graph shows that baseline frame time is consistently >16ms
   even without spikes, terrain flattening is likely necessary.
  4. Fog-of-war is 56% of sim cost. The headless stress test shows fog_of_war at 0.585ms/tick average. At 20 Hz sim rate
   that's only one tick every ~3 frames, but on tick frames it adds measurably. Worth investigating whether fog updates
  can be throttled further.
